Critical Conversations Toolkit

From time to time, you may need to have difficult but necessary conversations in the workplace. These conversations usually focus on confrontational topics such as poor performance, misconduct, a grievance or a dispute.

These conversations can be uncomfortable, but when they are done well, they have a positive impact on your team. You’ll solve problems with your employees, and the rest of your team will trust you more.

Critical Conversations Toolkit

This toolkit is designed to help you approach these conversations with confidence. The better prepared you are, the better equipped you are to handle conversations constructively and effectively. We’ve included tips, templates and other resources to help you.

Types of critical conversations

The first step is to identify what type of critical conversation you want to have. Critical conversations typically address unsatisfactory performance, but they could also include:

Extenuating personal circumstances

An employee is facing hardship. The manager and employee can discuss available avenues of support and options.

Grievance handling/potential misconduct/setting expectations

An example could be an employee has notified their manager of a colleague making offensive inappropriate jokes. The manager is to then meet with the individual to discuss expectations.

Unsuccessful promotion application

The manager needs to convey constructive feedback as to why the application was unsuccessful.

Unsuccessful recruitment application

The manager needs to provide feedback when a candidate is not successful.

Note: If you’re unsure if a critical conversation is necessary, talk to your Human Resources Business Partner. If there’s a serious ongoing concern related to performance or conduct, always consult with HR.

Prepare for the meeting

To make sure you’re in the right mindset, reflect and ask yourself a few questions. This will help you prepare and create a plan of action. It’s also the first step in deciding how you want to move forward.

If you jump in without a plan it can cause serious problems – would you know what to say if the other person doesn’t agree with you? How do you handle the meeting if there’s strong emotions? Critical conversations can be volatile if not handled properly.

Let’s see how you can prepare yourself.

Assessing the situation

Here are a few questions to help you look at the situation objectively. By taking a step back to think and reflect on the issue, it can help you outline the purpose of the conversation, your motivations and what you’d like to happen.

Tip: Remember, it’s important to stick to the facts and avoid subjective opinions or judgements.

What happened?

The first step is to reflect on the situation and what happened from your perspective. It’s also important to think back on your behaviour and how you may have contributed to the issue.

Here are a few questions to help map out the situation:

  • What is the issue?
  • What are the facts of the situation (who, what, when)? Identify specific examples of behaviour that link to the issue.
  • What has contributed to this issue arising? Including reflecting on your behaviour and how you may have contributed to the issue.
  • Have you discussed this issue with the staff member before? If so, what was the outcome?

Why is a conversation needed?

Why do you want to have a critical conversation? What’s your motivation?

Here are some examples of unhealthy and healthy motivators:

Unhealthy Motivators Healthy Motivators
  • I’m right, they need to see this.
  • I’m knowledgeable in this area and they should listen to my point of view.
  • I’m right.
  • They need to do what I expect them to.
  • They’re not doing what they are supposed to do, and they should be held to account or punished.
  • It’s their fault and they need to be held accountable.
  • Learn what is happening.
  • Understand their perspective.
  • Find the truth.
  • Solve problems.
  • Produce results.

Do you recognise your motives in either column?

It’s not about being right or telling the other person what they should do. If you feel some of the unhealthy examples are your motivators, you should consult with your HR Business Partner first so you can be guided and supported into having a constructive conversation.

What is the ideal outcome?

It’s important to decide on what you’d like the result of the conversation to be. It should link back to your motivators and be realistic. If you’re not sure if you’re being realistic, crosscheck with your HR Business Partner.

Ask yourself, what results do you want to see? What is the ideal outcome?

Some healthy examples are:

  • We want a shared understanding of the situation.
  • We want to agree on how best to move forward.
  • We want to find a way to work well together.
  • We want to agree on the way we each will behave moving forward.

Tip: For the ideal outcome examples, the language is inclusive by using ‘we’. Constructive conversations are about working together to find a solution as a team, not a ‘me vs you’ situation.

Should you be having the conversation?

Are you the best person to have this conversation? Would it be more appropriate for a more direct supervisor or is escalation required to a senior manager? Refer to your motivations and your ideal outcome, this should help you identify if you or another employee is better suited to having this critical conversation.

Consider if you have spoken to this person about this behaviour or issue before – do you need to escalate the issue? Does this need to become more formal – if so, involve your HR Business Partner?

Planning the conversation

Writing notes of what you want to say beforehand helps to cover the critical points in the conversation is useful. Use the Planning the Conversation template to keep you on track.

In the template you’ll find:

  • Your approach: How might you start the conversation?
  • Stick to the facts: How can you introduce the facts you have noticed?
  • Sharing your interpretation and listening to their interpretation: How will you introduce the way you’ve interpreted these facts? How can you ask the other person for their facts and their interpretation?
  • Your questions: What questions can you ask to help you understand the situation?
Tip: Always focus on the issue, not the person. Refer to their behaviour and actions. Don’t use generalised or personal criticisms. Be ready to hear a different interpretation of the facts than yours. It is easier to have the conversation if you have thought about the words you will use.

Considerations

There a few things you’ll need to consider:

Unconscious bias

How familiar with unconscious bias are you? Are there any conflicts of interest? Read through unconscious bias recognition and conflicts of interest.

Cultural sensitivities

Are there any cultural sensitivities? (e.g. Aboriginal Torres Strait Island considerations).

UOW policies and guidelines

Are you familiar with relevant policy and guidelines? Refer to Managing Performance and Conduct.

At this point, you should now be personally prepared for the conversation. Always consult with your HR Business Partner if you’re unsure about anything.

Let’s now move onto how to logistically prepare for the meeting.

Organise the meeting

After you’ve mapped out what you want to say, the next step is to organise the logistics of the meeting. This means deciding the right location, the right time and if others need to be included as well.

It’s important to set the scene on where and when to have the conversation. If you choose the wrong setting, it can backfire and possibly add more harm to the situation.

Where and when to have the conversation?

Location and privacy

Have it in a private space where others cannot see you or overhear. Also the person may become emotional and want privacy while they are talking to you.

Schedule appropriately

If it’s a serious conversation, it might be better to have it at the end of the day so the employee can go home instead of returning to work.

Allow enough time

Don’t book back-to-back meeting following this conversation or rush through the conversation to meet other commitments.

Give enough notice

Notify the employee at least 24 hours before the meeting and who will be attending. However, if it’s a formal meeting that may affect the employee’s employment, (and an HR Business Partner will be present), it is important to provide at least 48 hours’ notice.

Provide a clear purpose and, if necessary, allow the opportunity for the employee to include a support person in the meeting.

Employee Assistance Program (EAP) necessary?

If you feel that this conversation will put the employee or other employees’ mental wellbeing at risk, it can be helpful to provide them with contact details for the Employee Assistance Program (EAP).

Tip: Talk to your HR Business Partner if you’re unsure.

Who should be included?

If the meeting is a formal meeting that may affect the employee’s employment, it may be necessary to give the employee the opportunity to bring a support person.

Tip: A support person can be useful in meetings that deal with issues like performance management, misconduct, complex work or personal matters or other conversations that may be challenging for the employee.

What does the support person do?

The support person is present in the meeting for emotional support only. They must not act as an advocate for the employee or answer questions on the employee’s behalf. Make sure you explain this to the support person before the meeting.

Who can the support person be?

The employee can choose a friend, colleague, union official of the CPSU or the NTEU. They cannot be a practising barrister or solicitor in a private practice.

When would an HR Business Partner be involved?

An HR Business Partner would be involved if UOW needs to make a decision that impacts an employee’s employment. This includes but is not limited to:

  • performance management
  • misconduct investigations
  • change management
  • complex work or personal matters
  • absenteeism and
  • grievances.

Depending on the circumstances, it may also be reasonable for an HR Business Partner to be involved in informal meetings where the person may raise HR-related questions and needs support. If you’re inviting an HR Business Partner, you need to let the employee know before the meeting.

During the meeting

Now that you’ve organised the logistics of the meeting, let’s explore some tips and techniques to help you navigate the conversation.

Begin by stating your aim in having the discussion and your hope that you can both reach a shared understanding on how to move forward. Prepare your opening lines.

Be an active listener

This means giving your undivided attention to the other person. Listen to the words that are being said, and the person’s body language. Be attentive to your body language too – are you communicating openness or defensiveness and dominance? There’s more information in Resources on the five components of active listening .

Be respectful

Be mindful of your words and tone. Don’t use sentences that start with ‘you’, such as, ‘you’re always late’ or ‘you never meet deadlines’. These can sound like an attack and will most likely make the other person defensive, and the conversation will not be constructive. An alternative is to state the facts, for example, “I have noticed that every day last week you started after 9:15am”.

Avoid tangents

Stay on track with the purpose of the meeting. It’s helpful to have notes in front of you to remind you.

Be empathetic

If the employee becomes emotional, ask if they’d like to take a break. Also, if relevant, discuss any support or training that could assist the employee and prevent the issue arising in the future.

Seek to understand

Ask questions to understand the other person’s point of view. Ask if there are any mitigating circumstances, (work or non-work related), that need to be considered. Ask what solutions they’d like and try to find a resolution you can all agree to – this will help them feel committed to the solution. These questions are covered in the Planning the Conversation template.

Keeping the conversation healthy

Here are some helpful techniques to keep the conversation healthy.

  1. The ABC Model:
    • Acknowledge the points you both agree on.
    • Build on the points of agreement.
    • Compare your points of difference; is there any way you can merge your different perspectives together?

  2. The AMPP Model:

Click the items to read more.

A – Ask questions

Ask questions as they help you understand the other’s perspective:

  • “I’m not sure what you mean?”
  • “I’d like to understand your concerns?”
  • “Can you please tell me if you see it differently?”

M – Mirror

Mirror back to the person what you are hearing and seeing, to check understanding:

  • “You say that you’re okay but by your tone of voice, you seem upset. Is that right?”
  • “You seem nervous. Are you comfortable talking about this?”

P – Paraphrase

Paraphrase their response so they know you’ve heard them:

  • “Let’s see if I understand. You’re upset because I have voiced my concerns about some of the clothes you wear. From what I hear, you feel that this seems controlling or my ideas appear old fashioned to you. Is this right?”

P – Prime

Priming is saying what you believe they would say if they were totally comfortable with you.

  • “Maybe you’re thinking the only reason I am asking this is to save money?”
  • “Is it possible that you’re thinking…”

  1. Wrap up the conversation to keep your relationship healthy:
    • Ask them how you can support them while they are making the changes.
    • Discuss and agree how you will follow up with them on their progress.

After the meeting

Now let’s look at the next steps to take after the meeting:

Draft a written record

Write a summary of the meeting and the agreed actions resulting from the conversation. If an HR Business Partner attended the meeting, send your draft to them for review before sending to the employee.

Check in with the employee the next day as there may be a need for a further conversation – keep the door open.

Remain professional

Treat the employee as you normally would - don’t avoid or treat them differently.

Refer to your HR Business Partner

Inform your HR Business Partner of the outcome if they were involved in the planning process.

Constructive critical conversations can result in positive outcomes if you’re prepared and have support. It can lead to improved professional relationships and increase understanding between employees. Your overall aim should be to build trust, and to role model integrity and fairness.

Critical conversations are not easy! This toolkit is designed to set you up for success. Use the Resources dropdown menu at any time. If in doubt, refer to your HR Business Partner for support.
